
# Zion-TriCore V2 — Technical Whitepaper

## 1. Objective
To design a solid-state energy system that converts thermal radiation into electricity using near-field thermophotovoltaics (NF-TPV).

---

## 2. Physical Principles

### Thermal Radiation
Power emitted by a hot surface:

P = η(T) · σ · T⁴

Where:
- σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant
- T = temperature (Kelvin)

---

### Near-Field Enhancement
At nanoscale gaps, evanescent waves increase energy transfer beyond classical limits.

This allows significantly higher power density compared to far-field radiation.

---

## 3. System Components

### 3.1 Thermal Core
- Material: Alumina-Silicon Carbide (Al2O3-SiC)
- Function: Store and emit thermal energy

---

### 3.2 TPV Conversion Layer
- Material: GaSb / InGaAs
- Converts infrared radiation into electricity

---

### 3.3 Gap Control System
Maintains 100–500 nm spacing using:
- Piezoelectric actuators (PZT)
- Capacitive sensing
- PID control loop

---

### 3.4 Cooling System
- Galinstan liquid metal
- Maintains PV efficiency
- Enables heat recovery

---

## 4. Performance Estimates

| Metric | Estimated Value |
|------|----------------|
| Temperature | 1200–1600 K |
| Electrical Output | 5–20 W/cm² |
| Efficiency | 25–40% (TPV) |
| Total Efficiency | 70–85% (CHP) |

---

## 5. Engineering Challenges
- Maintaining nanoscale gap at high temperature
- Thermal expansion compensation
- Material durability under cycling
- Scaling the system

---

## 6. Conclusion
The TriCore V2 represents a promising approach to high-density solid-state energy systems but requires further simulation and experimental validation.
